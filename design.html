The goal of this study is to provide recommendations for the design of informational resources to bridge the gap between Theory and Design. Academic research provides important insights for product design, however, past work shows that academic findings are rarely applied in industry. I interviewed 18 design practitioners to identify what informational resources they use in their work and how these support their workflow. I prompted participants with design challenges, producing 18 outcomes which I compared to the interviews. Now, I am designing a website to assist designer's workflow based on my study's findings. It will work as a search engine to find solutions to specific design problems. The next step is conducting in-context usability tests of the tool.

This study provides design implications for helping designers create social comparisons that motivate higher game performance. Designers often make use of social comparisons to motivate people to perform better. I created two different feedbacks based on the concept of closeness to comparison. Specifically, I tested (1) comparing users to a target described as a similarly experienced player, and (2) adjusting the visual representation of performance so player scores appear closer to the comparison target. We evaluate the effects of these strategies for social comparison on player performance in a controlled experiment with 425 participants, followed by a survey. Both feedback techniques improved game performance, but only for experienced players. Read the note.
